Jurisdictional Considerations

IDRS accepts appointments across multiple jurisdictions, each governed by distinct legislative frameworks, institutional rules, and procedural requirements. An understanding of the applicable legal and institutional landscape is essential to the effective conduct of any dispute resolution process. The following provides an overview of the principal frameworks in each jurisdiction in which IDRS operates.

Asia-Pacific Jurisdictions

Australia

Australia's arbitration framework is governed by the International Arbitration Act 1974 (Cth) for international matters and state and territory Commercial Arbitration Acts for domestic disputes. Principal institutional rules include the ACICA Rules 2021 and the Resolution Institute Arbitration Rules 2023, together with the Resolution Institute Expert Determination Rules 2024. Additional dispute resolution mechanisms encompass mediation, state-specific security of payment legislation across all states and territories, dispute boards, and conciliation. Australia remains one of the most sophisticated arbitration seats in the Asia-Pacific region, with established judicial support for arbitral processes and a well-developed body of case law.

Hong Kong

Hong Kong's dispute resolution framework is underpinned by the Arbitration Ordinance (Cap. 609) and the Mediation Ordinance (Cap. 620). The Hong Kong International Arbitration Centre (HKIAC) administers arbitrations under its own Administered Arbitration Rules and is widely recognised as one of the leading arbitral institutions globally. Additional mechanisms include apology legislation, construction adjudication, and expert determination services. Hong Kong's position as a leading international arbitration seat is reinforced by its robust judicial infrastructure and the enforceability of arbitral awards under the New York Convention.

Singapore

Singapore's arbitration regime is governed by the International Arbitration Act (Cap. 143A) for international matters and the Arbitration Act (Cap. 10) for domestic disputes. The Singapore International Arbitration Centre (SIAC) administers proceedings under its own rules, while the Singapore International Mediation Centre (SIMC) provides structured mediation services. Singapore has adopted the Singapore Convention on Mediation and maintains the Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Act for construction-specific adjudication. Expedited arbitration options are available for disputes of moderate complexity, and Singapore is consistently ranked among the world's premier arbitration seats.

Malaysia

Malaysia's arbitration framework is founded upon the Arbitration Act 2005 (Act 646), as amended in 2018 and 2024, reflecting the country's commitment to modernising its dispute resolution infrastructure. The Asian International Arbitration Centre (AIAC) administers arbitrations under the AIAC Arbitration Rules 2026 and provides a range of alternative dispute resolution services. The Construction Industry Payment and Adjudication Act 2012 (CIPAA) provides statutory adjudication for construction disputes, while the Mediation Act 2012 establishes a framework for court-annexed and private mediation. Expert determination is also available as an independent dispute resolution mechanism.

China

China's arbitration framework is governed by the Arbitration Law of the People's Republic of China 1994, as amended in 2017. The China International Economic and Trade Arbitration Commission (CIETAC) administers proceedings under the CIETAC Rules 2024, and the Beijing Arbitration Commission provides additional institutional support. The Construction Law 1997 establishes the regulatory framework for construction disputes, while mediation-arbitration (med-arb) and People's Mediation provide culturally appropriate dispute resolution alternatives. China's Belt and Road dispute resolution mechanisms also provide specialised forums for cross-border infrastructure disputes.

India

India's arbitration regime is governed by the Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996, as amended in 2019, which introduced significant procedural reforms including time limits for the completion of arbitral proceedings. The Mumbai Centre for International Arbitration (MCIA) and the Delhi International Arbitration Centre (DIAC) serve as the principal arbitral institutions. The Commercial Courts Act 2015 establishes specialised courts for commercial disputes, while Lok Adalat conciliation and MSME facilitation provide additional dispute resolution pathways for appropriate matters.

Indonesia

Indonesia's arbitration framework is established by Law No. 30 of 1999 on Arbitration and Alternative Dispute Resolution. The Badan Arbitrase Nasional Indonesia (BANI) administers proceedings under the BANI Rules 2025, providing institutional support for both domestic and international arbitrations. The Construction Services Law 2017 governs construction-specific disputes, and mediation and expert determination services are available as complementary mechanisms across multiple sectors.

Japan

Japan's arbitration regime is governed by the Arbitration Act 2003 (Act No. 138), as amended in 2023 to align with the latest revisions to the UNCITRAL Model Law. The Japan Commercial Arbitration Association (JCAA) administers proceedings under its own rules, and the Japan International Dispute Resolution Center (JIDRC) provides additional institutional infrastructure. The Construction Business Act establishes Construction Dispute Review Committees for sector-specific disputes, while mediation (chotei) and conciliation provide further resolution pathways within the Japanese legal tradition.

Philippines

The Philippines' alternative dispute resolution framework is established by the Alternative Dispute Resolution Act 2004 (Republic Act No. 9285). The Philippine Dispute Resolution Center, Inc. (PDRCI) administers arbitrations under its own rules, while the Construction Industry Arbitration Commission (CIAC) has exclusive original jurisdiction over construction disputes pursuant to Executive Order 1008. Court-annexed mediation is available for matters within the court system, providing additional avenues for early resolution.

South Korea

South Korea's arbitration framework is governed by the Korean Arbitration Act (Act No. 6083), as amended in 2016. The Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (KCAB) administers proceedings under the KCAB International Arbitration Rules and provides comprehensive institutional support. The Framework Act on the Construction Industry establishes construction-specific dispute resolution mechanisms, and mediation services are available through multiple institutional pathways.

Taiwan

Taiwan's arbitration framework is governed by the Arbitration Act, as amended in 2015. The Chinese Arbitration Association (CAA) administers proceedings under its Arbitration Rules. The Construction Industry Act and the Public Construction Commission establish specialised dispute resolution mechanisms for construction and public works matters, while mediation and expert determination provide additional resolution pathways.

Thailand

Thailand's arbitration regime is governed by the Arbitration Act B.E. 2545 (2002), which adopts the UNCITRAL Model Law framework. The Thailand Arbitration Center (THAC) and the Thai Arbitration Institute administer proceedings under their respective rules. Mediation and expert determination services are available across commercial and construction sectors, with growing institutional support for alternative dispute resolution.

Vietnam

Vietnam's arbitration framework is established by the Law on Commercial Arbitration 2010 (Law 54/2010/QH12). The Vietnam International Arbitration Centre (VIAC) administers proceedings under the VIAC Rules, providing institutional support for both domestic and international disputes. The Construction Law 2014 governs construction-specific matters, and mediation and expert determination services are available as complementary mechanisms.

Europe & United Kingdom

United Kingdom

The United Kingdom's arbitration framework is governed by the Arbitration Act 1996, widely regarded as one of the most sophisticated arbitration statutes internationally. The London Court of International Arbitration (LCIA) and the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) administer proceedings under their respective rules from London. The Housing Grants, Construction and Regeneration Act 1996 establishes statutory adjudication for construction disputes, providing a rapid interim dispute resolution mechanism. The Technology and Construction Court, the Chartered Institute of Arbitrators (CIArb), and the Centre for Effective Dispute Resolution (CEDR) provide further institutional infrastructure supporting all forms of dispute resolution.

United States

The United States' arbitration framework is established by the Federal Arbitration Act 1925, supplemented by state-level Revised Uniform Arbitration Acts. The American Arbitration Association and International Centre for Dispute Resolution (AAA-ICDR) and JAMS administer proceedings under their respective rules. Mediation, Dispute Resolution Boards, and contract-specific provisions under the AIA and ConsensusDocs frameworks provide additional construction-specific mechanisms.

Canada

Canada's arbitration framework operates at both federal and provincial levels, with the Federal Commercial Arbitration Act providing the overarching structure and provincial legislation — including the British Columbia Arbitration Act 2020 and the Ontario Arbitration Act 1991 — governing domestic matters. The ADR Institute of Canada administers proceedings under its own rules. The Federal Prompt Payment for Construction Work Act 2023 and the Ontario Construction Act establish construction-specific adjudication and payment mechanisms, while mediation and expert determination are available across all provinces.
