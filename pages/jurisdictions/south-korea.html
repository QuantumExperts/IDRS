Jurisdictional Considerations

South Korea

Dispute Resolution Framework

South Korea has developed a sophisticated and modern dispute resolution framework reflecting its status as one of the world's leading economies and a major centre for technology and manufacturing. The South Korean dispute resolution landscape encompasses arbitration, mediation, litigation, and specialised mechanisms addressing construction and other industries. The jurisdiction's legal framework reflects civil law traditions combined with modern amendments aligning with international best practices in dispute resolution. South Korea's commitment to developing efficient dispute resolution mechanisms has established the jurisdiction as an important seat for international commercial arbitration, particularly for disputes involving Korean corporations and technology companies.

The South Korean approach to dispute resolution emphasises efficiency and effectiveness whilst respecting the rule of law and procedural fairness. The dispute resolution framework includes modern arbitration legislation, well-established arbitration institutions, court-integrated mediation and alternative dispute resolution mechanisms, and specialised procedures addressing construction disputes. The judiciary's active involvement in promoting dispute resolution efficiency and the development of specialist courts addressing particular categories of disputes reflect South Korea's comprehensive approach to dispute resolution.

Arbitration Legislation

The primary arbitration legislation in South Korea is the Korean Arbitration Act (Act No. 6083, amended 2016), which establishes a comprehensive legal framework for commercial arbitration. The Korean Arbitration Act incorporates principles from the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration and provides a modern legal foundation for arbitration in South Korea. The legislation addresses arbitration agreements, arbitrator appointment and qualifications, conduct of arbitral proceedings, interim relief, awards, and enforcement mechanisms. The Act provides that arbitration agreements are valid and enforceable and establishes procedures for arbitration proceedings reflecting contemporary best practices.

The 2016 amendments to the Korean Arbitration Act represented a significant modernisation of South Korea's arbitration legal framework. These amendments addressed matters including enhanced procedures for arbitrator appointment, improved interim relief mechanisms, streamlined award procedures, and clarified jurisdictional issues. The amendments demonstrate South Korea's commitment to maintaining a modern legal framework supporting international commercial arbitration and reflect the jurisdiction's desire to remain competitive in the international arbitration marketplace.

The Korean Arbitration Act establishes that parties may agree to arbitration and that arbitration agreements are enforceable contracts creating binding obligations to arbitrate covered disputes. The legislation provides broad freedom of contract regarding procedural matters and establishes that arbitrators may determine their own jurisdiction. The Act limits grounds for challenging awards and emphasises the finality of arbitral awards, reflecting contemporary approaches to arbitration law.

Institutional Frameworks

The Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (KCAB) serves as South Korea's primary arbitration institution and operates the KCAB International Arbitration Rules. KCAB administers both domestic and international commercial arbitrations and maintains headquarters in Seoul with regional offices in other Korean cities. The KCAB Rules provide comprehensive procedures for administered arbitrations and address all aspects of arbitration from initiation through award issuance. KCAB has positioned itself as the leading arbitration provider in South Korea and has worked to develop institutional capacity and procedures supporting international commercial arbitration.

KCAB maintains a roster of arbitrators with expertise spanning commercial, construction, technology, engineering, and other fields. The Centre provides full administrative services for arbitrations, including assistance with arbitrator appointment, hearing logistics, administrative cost management, and award procedures. KCAB has developed relationships with international arbitration practitioners and has promoted South Korea as an arbitration seat for international commercial disputes. The institution's development has contributed to South Korea's visibility in the international arbitration marketplace and has supported the development of arbitration practice within South Korea.

Beyond KCAB, dispute resolution services are provided by private arbitrators, mediation practitioners, and alternative dispute resolution providers. The availability of alternative providers creates a competitive marketplace in which parties may select providers suited to their dispute resolution requirements and preferences.

Mediation Frameworks

Mediation is recognised in South Korea as an important alternative dispute resolution mechanism and is promoted through statutory frameworks and court integration. The Alternative Dispute Resolution Act in South Korea establishes the legal framework for mediation and provides statutory recognition of mediation as a formal dispute resolution mechanism. The legislation addresses mediator qualifications, mediation procedures, confidentiality, and the enforceability of mediation settlements. The statutory framework provides clarity regarding mediation processes and supports the utilisation of mediation as an alternative to litigation and arbitration.

The South Korean courts have integrated mediation into civil procedure and actively promote mediation to parties before trial. The courts maintain mediation facilities and trained mediators facilitate settlement discussions. The courts' promotion of mediation has contributed to increased utilisation of mediation and has influenced professional attitudes towards mediation. The availability of court-annexed mediation services has made mediation more accessible to parties and has contributed to high settlement rates in the Korean courts.

Institutional mediation services in South Korea are provided by KCAB and various other organisations offering mediation services. Mediators are trained in mediation techniques and are drawn from diverse professional backgrounds, including law, commerce, engineering, and technology. The availability of institutional mediation services and the statutory framework supporting mediation have contributed to the development of a professional mediation community in South Korea.

Construction and Infrastructure Framework

The Framework Act on Construction Industry establishes the legal framework regulating the construction industry in South Korea and addresses matters including construction contracting, licensing, quality standards, and dispute resolution. The legislation recognises arbitration and mediation as appropriate mechanisms for resolving construction disputes and provides that construction disputes may be referred to arbitration. The legislation reflects the importance of the construction industry to South Korea's economy and the need for effective dispute resolution mechanisms addressing construction disputes.

Construction dispute resolution in South Korea utilises arbitration mechanisms provided through KCAB, court-based litigation, and contractual dispute resolution procedures. The construction industry increasingly incorporates Dispute Boards and other interim determination mechanisms into major construction contracts, particularly contracts involving international contractors. These mechanisms enable rapid resolution of interim disputes whilst construction proceeds, protecting schedule and cash flow. The integration of dispute resolution mechanisms into construction contracts reflects international best practice and is increasingly common in major Korean construction projects.

The Korea Arbitration Board also maintains specialised procedures for construction arbitration and operates with expertise in addressing construction disputes. This specialised focus supports the effective resolution of construction disputes and reflects the Korean approach to developing industry-specific dispute resolution procedures.

Expert Determination

Expert determination is recognised in South Korea as a mechanism for resolving technical disputes. Expert determinations may be conducted pursuant to contractual frameworks or through institutional arrangements. The professional services sector in South Korea includes highly qualified expert determiners with expertise in construction engineering, architecture, technology, commercial valuation, and other specialised fields. The availability of qualified expert determiners has supported the utilisation of expert determination for resolving technical disputes in construction, technology, and commercial contexts.

Enforcement and Recognition

South Korea is a signatory to the Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958 and awards made in foreign jurisdictions are enforceable in South Korea. The procedures for enforcement of foreign awards are established in the Korean Arbitration Act and in rules of court procedure. The South Korean courts have adopted a supportive approach to enforcement of foreign awards, subject to compliance with Korean law and public policy. The courts maintain limited grounds for refusing enforcement consistent with the New York Convention requirements.

Awards made in South Korean arbitrations are enforceable through the South Korean courts. The enforcement process requires application to the court with jurisdiction over the party against whom enforcement is sought or over the subject matter of the dispute. The courts may refuse enforcement only where enforcement would violate public policy or where the arbitration was conducted in violation of fundamental procedural requirements. The South Korean courts' approach to arbitration enforcement emphasises the finality of arbitral awards and limits judicial intervention in arbitral matters.

Mediation and conciliation settlements are enforceable as settlement agreements and may be enforced through court procedures. The statutory framework supports the enforceability of mediation settlements and provides parties with confidence regarding the legal effect of agreements reached through mediation.

Conclusion

South Korea has developed a sophisticated and modern dispute resolution framework reflecting its status as one of the world's leading economies and a major centre for international commerce and technology. The Korean Arbitration Act 2016 amendments, the development of KCAB as a leading arbitration institution, the integration of mediation into the court system, and the development of specialised construction dispute resolution procedures demonstrate South Korea's comprehensive approach to dispute resolution. South Korea's commitment to maintaining modern legal frameworks and institutional infrastructure supporting efficient dispute resolution has established the jurisdiction as an important seat for international commercial arbitration. For parties seeking to resolve disputes involving South Korean parties or relating to projects or transactions in South Korea, the jurisdiction provides modern legal frameworks, well-established institutional infrastructure, and a professional dispute resolution community supporting efficient and effective dispute resolution.
