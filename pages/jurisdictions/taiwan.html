Jurisdictional Considerations

Taiwan

Dispute Resolution Framework

Taiwan has developed a comprehensive dispute resolution framework reflecting its status as a major centre for manufacturing, technology, and financial services. The Taiwanese dispute resolution landscape encompasses arbitration, mediation, conciliation, and litigation, underpinned by clear statutory frameworks and a judiciary supportive of alternative dispute resolution mechanisms. Taiwan's legal system reflects civil law traditions combined with modern amendments aligning with international best practices in dispute resolution. The jurisdiction's commitment to developing efficient dispute resolution mechanisms and supporting institutional infrastructure has established Taiwan as an attractive seat for international commercial arbitration, particularly for disputes involving Taiwanese companies in the technology, manufacturing, and electronics sectors.

The Taiwanese approach to dispute resolution emphasises efficiency and effectiveness whilst respecting procedural fairness. The dispute resolution framework includes modern arbitration legislation, well-established arbitration institutions, court-integrated alternative dispute resolution mechanisms, and specialised procedures addressing construction and other industry-specific disputes. The judiciary's support for alternative dispute resolution and the development of professional dispute resolution infrastructure reflect Taiwan's comprehensive approach to providing effective dispute resolution services.

Arbitration Legislation

The primary arbitration legislation in Taiwan is the Arbitration Act (as amended in 2015), which establishes a comprehensive legal framework for commercial arbitration. This legislation provides the statutory foundation for arbitration in Taiwan and incorporates principles reflecting the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration. The Arbitration Act addresses arbitration agreements, arbitrator appointment and qualifications, conduct of arbitral proceedings, interim relief, awards, and enforcement mechanisms. The legislation provides that arbitration agreements are valid and enforceable and establishes procedures for arbitration reflecting contemporary best practices.

The 2015 amendments to the Arbitration Act represented a significant modernisation of Taiwan's arbitration legal framework. These amendments addressed matters including enhanced interim relief procedures, clarified jurisdictional matters, streamlined award procedures, and improved mechanisms for enforcing awards. The amendments demonstrate Taiwan's commitment to maintaining a modern and competitive arbitration legal framework and reflect the jurisdiction's desire to remain attractive as an arbitration seat for international commercial disputes.

The Arbitration Act establishes that parties may agree to arbitrate disputes and that arbitration agreements are valid and enforceable. The legislation provides broad freedom of contract regarding procedural matters and establishes that arbitrators may determine their own jurisdiction. The Act limits grounds for challenging awards and emphasises the finality of arbitral awards, reflecting contemporary approaches to arbitration law and demonstrating Taiwan's commitment to supporting arbitration as an efficient alternative to litigation.

Institutional Frameworks

The Chinese Arbitration Association (CAA) serves as Taiwan's primary arbitration institution and operates the CAA Arbitration Rules. CAA administers both domestic and international commercial arbitrations and maintains headquarters in Taipei. The CAA Rules provide comprehensive procedures for administered arbitrations and address all aspects of the arbitration process from initial registration through award issuance. CAA has positioned itself as Taiwan's leading arbitration provider and has worked to develop institutional capacity and procedures supporting international commercial arbitration.

CAA maintains a roster of arbitrators with expertise spanning commercial, construction, technology, engineering, and other fields. The Centre provides full administrative services for arbitrations, including assistance with arbitrator appointment, hearing arrangement, and award procedures. CAA has developed relationships with international arbitration practitioners and has promoted Taiwan as an arbitration seat for regional and international disputes. The institution's development has contributed to Taiwan's visibility in the international arbitration marketplace and has supported the growth of arbitration practice within Taiwan.

Beyond CAA, dispute resolution services are provided by private arbitrators, mediators, and alternative dispute resolution providers. The availability of alternative providers creates a competitive marketplace in which parties may select providers suited to their dispute resolution requirements and preferences.

Mediation Frameworks

Mediation is recognised in Taiwan as an important alternative dispute resolution mechanism and is promoted through statutory frameworks and court integration. The Mediation Act in Taiwan establishes the legal framework for mediation and provides statutory recognition of mediation as a formal dispute resolution mechanism. The legislation addresses mediator qualifications, mediation procedures, confidentiality protections, and the enforceability of mediation settlements. The statutory framework provides clarity regarding mediation processes and encourages utilisation of mediation as an alternative to litigation and arbitration.

The Taiwanese courts have integrated mediation into civil procedure and actively promote mediation to parties before trial. The courts maintain mediation facilities and trained mediators facilitate settlement discussions. The courts' promotion of mediation has contributed to increased utilisation of mediation and has influenced professional attitudes towards mediation. The availability of court-annexed mediation services has made mediation more accessible to parties and has contributed to high settlement rates in the Taiwanese courts.

Institutional mediation services in Taiwan are provided by CAA and various other organisations offering mediation services. Mediators are trained in mediation techniques and are drawn from diverse professional backgrounds, including law, business, engineering, and technology. The availability of institutional mediation services and the statutory framework supporting mediation have contributed to the development of a professional mediation community in Taiwan.

Construction Industry Framework

Taiwan has enacted the Construction Industry Act, which establishes regulation of the construction industry and addresses matters including construction contracting, contractor licensing, quality assurance, and dispute resolution. The legislation recognises arbitration and mediation as appropriate mechanisms for resolving construction disputes. The legislation reflects Taiwan's recognition that the construction industry requires specialised dispute resolution frameworks and that efficient mechanisms for addressing construction disputes are important to the industry's development.

The Public Construction Commission operates dispute resolution procedures addressing disputes in publicly-funded construction projects. These procedures address disputes arising in government construction contracts and provide mechanisms for rapid resolution of construction disputes on public works projects. The Commission's framework reflects Taiwan's approach to developing specialised procedures addressing particular categories of construction disputes.

The construction industry in Taiwan utilises arbitration, mediation, and contractual dispute resolution mechanisms for addressing disputes. The use of Dispute Boards and other interim determination mechanisms is increasingly incorporated into major construction contracts, particularly those involving international contractors. These mechanisms enable rapid resolution of interim disputes whilst construction proceeds, protecting schedule and cash flow.

Expert Determination

Expert determination is recognised in Taiwan as a mechanism for resolving disputes involving technical or specialised matters. Expert determinations may be conducted pursuant to contractual frameworks or through institutional arrangements. The professional services sector in Taiwan includes highly qualified expert determiners with expertise in construction engineering, architecture, property matters, technology valuation, and other specialised fields. The availability of qualified expert determiners has supported the utilisation of expert determination for resolving technical disputes in construction, technology, and commercial contexts.

The Taiwanese courts have recognised expert determinations made within the expert's jurisdiction as binding determinations, provided they comply with procedural fairness requirements and do not involve fraud or manifest error. This judicial recognition supports the utilisation of expert determination and provides parties with confidence that determinations made by qualified experts will be enforced by the courts where appropriate.

Enforcement and Recognition

Taiwan, as a jurisdiction that is not a signatory to the Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958, operates under a distinct enforcement regime. However, Taiwan has developed reciprocal recognition and enforcement arrangements with other jurisdictions and has established procedures for enforcing foreign arbitral awards through domestic law. The Taiwanese courts have adopted a generally supportive approach to enforcement of foreign awards where reciprocal arrangements exist or where the awards do not violate Taiwanese law or public policy.

Awards made in Taiwanese arbitrations are enforceable through the Taiwanese courts. The enforcement process requires application to the court with jurisdiction over the party against whom enforcement is sought. The courts may refuse enforcement only where enforcement would violate public policy or where the arbitration was conducted in violation of fundamental procedural requirements. The Taiwanese courts' approach to arbitration enforcement emphasises the finality of arbitral awards and limits judicial intervention in arbitral matters.

Mediation and conciliation settlements are enforceable as settlement agreements and may be enforced through court procedures. The statutory framework supports the enforceability of mediation settlements and provides parties with confidence regarding the legal effect of agreements reached through mediation.

Conclusion

Taiwan has developed a comprehensive dispute resolution framework reflecting its status as a major centre for technology and manufacturing and its role as an important participant in international commerce. The Arbitration Act 2015 amendments, the development of CAA as a leading arbitration institution, the integration of mediation into the court system, and the development of specialised procedures addressing construction disputes demonstrate Taiwan's commitment to providing effective dispute resolution services. Taiwan's legal frameworks and institutional infrastructure support efficient resolution of disputes involving Taiwanese parties and disputes relating to projects or transactions in Taiwan. For parties seeking to resolve disputes in the Taiwan context, the jurisdiction provides modern legal frameworks and established institutional infrastructure supporting effective dispute resolution through arbitration, mediation, and other alternative mechanisms.
