Jurisdictional Considerations

Indonesia

Dispute Resolution Framework

Indonesia, as one of Southeast Asia's largest economies and home to the world's fourth-largest population, has developed a dispute resolution framework addressing the needs of its substantial domestic market and increasing international commercial engagement. The Indonesian dispute resolution landscape encompasses arbitration, mediation, conciliation, and litigation, underpinned by a civil law legal tradition influenced by Dutch legal heritage combined with Islamic legal principles. Indonesia's dispute resolution framework has undergone modernisation to support international commercial arbitration and to address the needs of major domestic and international projects operating within the jurisdiction.

The Indonesian dispute resolution system is characterised by a mix of formal legal frameworks, institutional structures, and traditional dispute resolution practices. The jurisdiction's role in regional supply chains, infrastructure development, and investment has increased demand for efficient and sophisticated dispute resolution mechanisms. Indonesia's commitment to developing its institutional infrastructure for dispute resolution, whilst maintaining coherence with its civil law traditions, reflects recognition of the importance of effective dispute resolution to commercial confidence and investment attraction.

Arbitration Legislation

The principal arbitration legislation in Indonesia is Law No. 30 of 1999 Regarding Arbitration and Alternative Dispute Resolution (Law 30/1999), which establishes the legal framework for commercial arbitration in Indonesia. This legislation provides the statutory foundation for arbitration agreements, the conduct of arbitral proceedings, interim relief, awards, and enforcement mechanisms. The legislation distinguishes between domestic and international arbitrations, though many of its core principles apply to both categories. The legislative framework reflects Indonesia's approach to promoting arbitration as an efficient alternative to litigation.

Law 30/1999 provides that arbitration agreements are enforceable and create binding obligations to arbitrate covered disputes. The legislation requires that arbitration must be conducted before an arbitration body or arbitrators appointed in accordance with the provisions of the Act. The Act provides detailed procedures for arbitration proceedings and establishes that awards are final and binding and may only be set aside in limited circumstances specified in the legislation. The legislative framework emphasises the finality of arbitral awards and limits court intervention in arbitral proceedings.

The scope of arbitrable disputes under Indonesian law encompasses commercial disputes including disputes arising from contracts relating to trade, commerce, and business transactions. Disputes arising in criminal matters, family law, or concerning rights that cannot be freely disposed of by the parties are excluded from arbitration. This approach to arbitrability is consistent with the civil law tradition and reflects Indonesian policy regarding which matters are appropriate for private arbitration versus governmental regulation or court jurisdiction.

Institutional Frameworks

The primary arbitration institution in Indonesia is the Indonesian National Arbitration Board (Badan Arbitrase Nasional Indonesia, BANI), which operates the BANI Arbitration Rules 2025. BANI administers commercial arbitrations and maintains headquarters in Jakarta with regional offices in other Indonesian cities. BANI operates administered arbitration services for parties seeking institutional administration of their arbitrations. The BANI Rules 2025 provide comprehensive procedures for administered arbitrations and reflect contemporary developments in international arbitration practice.

BANI maintains a roster of arbitrators with expertise spanning diverse commercial, construction, engineering, and technical fields. The Centre provides full administrative services for administered arbitrations, including assistance with arbitrator appointment, hearing arrangement, award issuance, and cost administration. BANI has positioned itself as the leading arbitration institution in Indonesia and has worked to develop its institutional capacity and procedural rules to support international commercial arbitration. The Centre's role in administering arbitrations in Indonesia has made it an important institution for parties undertaking arbitrations involving Indonesian parties or relating to Indonesian projects.

Beyond BANI, dispute resolution services in Indonesia are provided by various other institutions and independent practitioners. The availability of institutional alternatives and independent arbitrators creates a competitive marketplace in which parties may select providers suited to their dispute resolution requirements and preferences.

Mediation Frameworks

Mediation is recognised under Law 30/1999 as a form of alternative dispute resolution and is regulated alongside arbitration and conciliation. The legislation establishes procedures for mediation, including requirements regarding mediator qualifications, mediation procedures, and the enforceability of mediation agreements. Mediation may be conducted as a precursor to arbitration or as a standalone mechanism, depending on parties' preferences and contractual arrangements. The statutory framework provides clarity regarding mediation procedures and establishes the legal effect of mediation settlements.

Institutional mediation services in Indonesia are provided by BANI and various other organisations offering mediation services. Mediators are selected from diverse professional backgrounds and are trained in mediation techniques. The availability of institutional mediation services and the statutory framework supporting mediation have contributed to increased utilisation of mediation as a dispute resolution mechanism in Indonesia. The integration of mediation into the broader alternative dispute resolution framework supports parties' access to consensual dispute resolution mechanisms.

The Indonesian courts have increasingly promoted mediation as an alternative to traditional litigation. Judges are encouraged to facilitate settlement discussions and to promote parties' consideration of mediation and other alternative dispute resolution mechanisms prior to trial. The courts' support for mediation reflects governmental policy encouraging consensual dispute resolution and reflects recognition of the benefits of settlement in reducing court congestion and improving access to justice.

Construction Services and Industry Framework

Indonesia has enacted the Construction Services Law 2017, which establishes comprehensive regulation of construction services and addresses matters including construction contracting, licensing, liability, and dispute resolution. The legislation recognises arbitration and mediation as appropriate mechanisms for resolving construction disputes and establishes that construction disputes may be referred to arbitration or mediation. The legislation reflects the importance of construction activity to Indonesia's development and recognises the need for effective dispute resolution mechanisms addressing construction disputes.

The construction industry in Indonesia utilises arbitration, mediation, and traditional contractual dispute resolution mechanisms for addressing disputes. Major construction projects often incorporate Dispute Boards or other rapid determination mechanisms to address disputes during construction. These mechanisms enable ongoing construction work whilst disputes are resolved, protecting project schedule and cash flow. The utilisation of streamlined dispute resolution mechanisms in construction contracts reflects international best practice and is increasingly common in major Indonesian construction projects.

Adjudication-style mechanisms are increasingly incorporated into construction contracts in Indonesia, particularly in projects involving international contractors or international financing. These mechanisms provide rapid determination of interim disputes and enable construction to continue whilst disputes are being resolved. The development of construction-specific dispute resolution frameworks reflects the importance of the construction industry to Indonesia's economy and the need for efficient mechanisms addressing construction disputes.

Expert Determination

Expert determination is recognised in Indonesia as a mechanism for resolving technical disputes. Expert determinations may be conducted pursuant to contractual frameworks or may be administered through institutional frameworks. The professional services sector in Indonesia includes expert determiners with expertise in construction engineering, architecture, property matters, commercial evaluation, and other specialised fields. The availability of qualified expert determiners has supported the utilisation of expert determination in resolving technical disputes in construction, property, and commercial contexts.

The Indonesian courts have recognised expert determinations as valid mechanisms for resolving disputes and have generally enforced determinations made within the expert's jurisdiction and in accordance with procedural fairness requirements. This judicial recognition of expert determination has encouraged its utilisation and has provided parties with confidence that determinations made by qualified experts will be recognised by the courts.

Enforcement and Recognition

Indonesia is a signatory to the Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958 (the New York Convention) and awards made in foreign jurisdictions are enforceable in Indonesia. The procedures for enforcement of foreign awards are established in Law 30/1999 and in procedural rules promulgated by the Indonesian courts. The Indonesian courts have adopted a generally supportive approach to enforcement of foreign awards, subject to compliance with Indonesian law and public policy. The courts have limited grounds for refusing enforcement consistent with the New York Convention requirements.

Arbitral awards made in Indonesia are enforceable through the Indonesian courts. The enforcement process requires application to the District Court (Pengadilan Negeri) with jurisdiction over the party against whom enforcement is sought or over the subject matter of the dispute. The courts may refuse enforcement where the award violates public policy (ordre public) or where the arbitration was conducted in violation of the fundamental procedural requirements of Law 30/1999. The concept of public policy in Indonesian law encompasses matters of fundamental legal principle and public interest.

Mediation settlements are enforceable through contractual mechanisms and, where parties have agreed, through recognition as settlement agreements enforceable through court proceedings. The statutory framework supports the enforceability of mediation settlements and provides parties with confidence regarding the legal effect of agreements reached through mediation.

Conclusion

Indonesia's dispute resolution framework reflects the jurisdiction's development needs and its commitment to supporting commercial dispute resolution through arbitration and mediation. The legislative framework established by Law 30/1999, the development of BANI as a leading arbitration institution, and the integration of dispute resolution mechanisms into major legal frameworks such as the Construction Services Law 2017 demonstrate Indonesia's comprehensive approach to dispute resolution. Indonesia's role as a major regional economy and the substantial volume of international commercial activity in the jurisdiction have established effective dispute resolution mechanisms as important to commercial confidence and investment attraction. For parties seeking to resolve disputes involving Indonesian parties or relating to projects or transactions in Indonesia, the jurisdiction provides established legal frameworks and institutional structures supporting efficient dispute resolution.
