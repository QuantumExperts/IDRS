Jurisdictional Considerations

China

Dispute Resolution Framework

China has developed a comprehensive dispute resolution framework reflecting the unique characteristics of the People's Republic of China's legal system. The Chinese dispute resolution landscape encompasses arbitration, mediation, litigation, and other mechanisms and is characterised by substantial governmental involvement in dispute resolution processes. The jurisdiction's approach to dispute resolution reflects Confucian principles emphasising harmony and reconciliation, combined with modern statutory frameworks addressing commercial disputes. China's role as one of the world's leading economic powers and its participation in major international infrastructure projects through the Belt and Road Initiative have established Chinese dispute resolution frameworks as increasingly important for international commercial disputes.

The Chinese dispute resolution framework operates within a context of substantial governmental oversight and policy direction. Government agencies, state-owned enterprises, and private entities all participate in China's dispute resolution marketplace. The dispute resolution landscape includes formal arbitration institutions, mediation services provided by governmental and non-governmental organisations, and court-based litigation. Understanding the intersection of formal legal frameworks with governmental policy and practice is essential for parties engaging in dispute resolution in China.

Arbitration Legislation

The primary arbitration legislation in China is the Arbitration Law 1994 (promulgated 1994), as amended by the Arbitration Law Amendment 2017. This legislation establishes the legal framework for commercial arbitration in China and applies to both domestic and international commercial arbitrations. The Arbitration Law reflects the PRC's approach to dispute resolution and establishes that arbitration is final and binding, subject to limited grounds for setting aside awards. The legislation emphasises the finality of arbitral awards and provides clear procedures for arbitration proceedings.

The Arbitration Law establishes the legal framework for arbitration agreements, appointment of arbitrators, conduct of proceedings, confidentiality, and award procedures. The legislation provides that arbitration agreements must be in writing and that arbitration must be conducted before an arbitration commission with jurisdiction over the subject matter of the dispute. The structure of arbitration in China differs from Western arbitration frameworks in that arbitrations must be conducted under the auspices of a formally established arbitration commission rather than as purely ad hoc proceedings. This institutional approach reflects China's preference for structured, supervised dispute resolution processes.

The Arbitration Law has been refined through judicial interpretation and administrative guidance issued by the Supreme People's Court of the PRC. These interpretations address matters including the validity of arbitration agreements, the scope of arbitral jurisdiction, interim relief procedures, and the grounds for setting aside awards. The judicial interpretations reflect the courts' approach to arbitration and provide guidance to arbitration commissions and practitioners regarding the application of the Arbitration Law.

Institutional Frameworks

The primary arbitration institutions in China include the China International Economic and Trade Arbitration Commission (CIETAC) and the Beijing Arbitration Commission (BAC). CIETAC operates the CIETAC Arbitration Rules 2024 and is the leading institution for international commercial arbitration involving Chinese parties. CIETAC maintains arbitration commissions in Beijing, Shanghai, and Shenzhen and administers a substantial volume of international commercial arbitrations. The CIETAC Rules 2024 represent the current framework for CIETAC arbitrations and reflect contemporary best practice in arbitration administration.

The Beijing Arbitration Commission operates the BAC Arbitration Rules and serves as the primary institution for arbitration involving Beijing-based parties and disputes arising in the Beijing area. The BAC administers both international and domestic arbitrations and maintains a substantial roster of arbitrators. Other regional arbitration commissions operate in Shanghai, Guangzhou, and other major Chinese cities, serving parties with disputes relating to those regions. The network of regional arbitration commissions reflects the decentralised nature of China's arbitration system and ensures that arbitration services are available throughout the jurisdiction.

CIETAC has invested substantially in developing its rules and procedures and has established itself as one of China's most prominent dispute resolution institutions. The Centre maintains modern hearing facilities in Beijing, Shanghai, and Shenzhen and has developed relationships with international arbitration practitioners. CIETAC administers a substantial volume of international arbitrations and has become increasingly prominent in international arbitration circles. The availability of CIETAC as an administered arbitration provider has contributed to China's attractiveness as an arbitration seat for international commercial disputes.

Mediation Frameworks

Mediation occupies a central role in China's dispute resolution framework, reflecting the influence of Confucian principles on dispute resolution practice. The mediation system in China encompasses People's Mediation, administered mediation by mediation organisations, and mediation provided by courts. People's Mediation, conducted by trained community mediators, has deep roots in Chinese culture and represents a traditional mechanism for resolving disputes at the grassroots level. This system continues to operate and serves important functions in addressing disputes in communities and small-scale commercial contexts.

Mediation organisations provide administered mediation services addressing commercial, family, and civil disputes. These organisations operate pursuant to legislation and maintain rosters of trained mediators. The mediation rules established by mediation organisations address procedures for initiating mediation, conduct of mediation sessions, confidentiality, and the enforceability of mediation settlements. Mediation settlements that satisfy statutory requirements are enforceable through court proceedings, providing parties with certainty regarding the legal effect of mediation agreements.

The courts in China actively promote mediation and have integrated mediation into civil procedure. Judges are trained in mediation techniques and may conduct mediation discussions with parties prior to judgment. The courts' promotion of mediation reflects governmental policy preferences supporting consensual dispute resolution and reflects the continuing influence of Confucian reconciliation principles on China's approach to dispute resolution. The availability of court-annexed mediation has contributed to high settlement rates in the Chinese courts and has reduced the volume of cases proceeding to judgment.

Construction and Mediation-Arbitration

The Construction Law 1997 (as amended) establishes legal frameworks for construction industry regulation and dispute resolution. This legislation addresses matters including construction contracts, construction licensing requirements, quality assurance, and dispute resolution. The legislation provides that disputes relating to construction projects may be resolved through mediation, arbitration, or litigation. Construction industry participants have increasingly utilised mediation and arbitration for resolving construction disputes, reducing reliance on court-based litigation.

The med-arb mechanism, combining mediation with arbitration, has become increasingly utilised in Chinese dispute resolution practice. Under this mechanism, parties first attempt to resolve their dispute through mediation and, if mediation does not result in settlement, the matter proceeds to arbitration. This sequential approach reflects Chinese policy preferences supporting mediation whilst ensuring that parties have access to binding determination if mediation does not succeed. The med-arb mechanism has proven popular in China and has become a standard feature of many dispute resolution frameworks operating in the jurisdiction.

People's Mediation conducted at the grassroots level plays a significant role in addressing construction disputes in communities and small-scale projects. People's mediators, trained community members drawn from local populations, provide mediation services addressing neighbourhood disputes, minor construction disputes, and other community-level conflicts. This system reflects traditional dispute resolution approaches and continues to serve important functions in Chinese society, complementing formal legal systems.

Belt and Road Dispute Resolution

China has given substantial attention to developing dispute resolution mechanisms supporting the Belt and Road Initiative, the massive infrastructure and development programme spanning Asia, Africa, Europe, and the Americas. Recognising that effective dispute resolution mechanisms are essential for managing disputes arising in Belt and Road projects, Chinese governmental and institutional actors have developed specialised frameworks and have promoted the utilisation of Chinese arbitration institutions for resolving Belt and Road disputes. These efforts reflect China's strategic interest in positioning Chinese dispute resolution institutions as preferred seats for international arbitrations involving Belt and Road projects.

CIETAC and other Chinese arbitration institutions have developed specialist procedures for Belt and Road arbitrations and have marketed their services to parties involved in Belt and Road projects. These institutions have promoted the utilisation of Chinese law and Chinese arbitration seats for resolving Belt and Road disputes, leveraging the substantial volumes of Belt and Road disputes anticipated as projects proceed. The development of Belt and Road dispute resolution frameworks reflects China's strategic approach to utilising its dispute resolution infrastructure to support the Belt and Road Initiative and to advance Chinese interests in international commercial and investment disputes.

Expert Determination

Expert determination is recognised in China as a valid mechanism for resolving technical disputes. Expert determinations may be conducted pursuant to contractual frameworks or may be conducted under the auspices of mediation organisations or other institutional providers. The professional services sector in China includes experts spanning construction engineering, architecture, property valuation, commercial matters, and other specialised fields. These experts bring substantial industry experience and technical expertise to their determinations, enabling efficient resolution of technical disputes.

Enforcement and Recognition

China is a signatory to the Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958 (the New York Convention) and awards made in foreign jurisdictions are enforceable in China subject to the procedures and grounds specified in the Arbitration Law and the New York Convention. Chinese courts have established procedures for recognising and enforcing foreign arbitral awards and have generally adopted a supportive approach to enforcement, subject to the requirement that awards do not violate Chinese law or public policy. The practical enforcement of foreign awards in China can present challenges and requires careful attention to procedural requirements and substantive compliance with Chinese law.

Arbitral awards made in China are enforceable through the Chinese courts. The enforcement process requires application to the court with jurisdiction over the party against whom enforcement is sought. The courts may set aside awards on limited grounds, including breach of procedure, excess of jurisdiction, fraud, or violations of public policy. The courts' approach to setting aside awards is generally deferential to the arbitral process, reflecting legislative policy supporting the finality of arbitral awards. However, the concept of "public policy" in Chinese law encompasses a broader range of considerations than in some Western jurisdictions and may provide courts with additional grounds for refusing enforcement in certain circumstances.

Conclusion

China's dispute resolution framework reflects the unique characteristics of the PRC's legal system and its approach to governance and dispute resolution. The framework encompasses formal arbitration institutions, mediation processes reflecting traditional reconciliation principles, and court-based litigation. The development of specialised frameworks supporting Belt and Road dispute resolution reflects China's strategic approach to leveraging dispute resolution in support of economic and political objectives. For parties engaging in dispute resolution involving Chinese parties or relating to projects in China, understanding the distinctive characteristics of China's dispute resolution system is essential. The complexity of the Chinese legal framework, the involvement of governmental actors and policy considerations, and the integration of mediation with other dispute resolution mechanisms create a distinctive environment for dispute resolution in China.
